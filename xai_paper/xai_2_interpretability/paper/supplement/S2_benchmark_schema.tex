\section{Benchmark schema and glossary}\label{supp:schema}

The leaderboard is built from a flat record schema, and stating it removes the ambiguity
about what a single number on the benchmark counts. Each method is run per game and writes a
per-game record under its phase's \texttt{out/} tree. The leaderboard aggregates those
records into one row per method by averaging over games. The schema columns of a leaderboard
row are given in Table~\ref{supp:tab:schema}. The aggregation rule is a mean over the
$n_{\text{games}}$ games, with the per-game records first reduced within a game when a method
emits several output records. The faithfulness, sufficiency, and minimality columns
report the F, S, and M axes defined in \Sectionname3.4. We do not re-derive those definitions here.

The vocabulary that the schema implies is worth fixing precisely, because the review asked
for a single unambiguous method count. We distinguish a \emph{method} (one interpretability
technique, e.g.\ \texttt{vanilla\_saliency}), a \emph{method row} (its single aggregated line
on the leaderboard), a \emph{per-game record} (one method scored on one game, written under a
phase \texttt{out/} tree), an \emph{output record} (a per-game record for one output regime,
several of which a method may emit on one game), a \emph{family mean} (the average over the
members of a tradition family, e.g.\ causal/intervention), the \emph{oracle positive
control} (the exact $\mathrm{do}(u)$ ground truth of \Sectionname3.2, scored as a method to anchor the
ceiling), and a \emph{not-applicable method} (one that does not apply to a given output
regime and is recorded as N/A rather than as a zero). With this vocabulary the count is
unambiguous. The benchmark scores 30 interpretability methods together with the intervention
oracle as a positive control, for 31 leaderboard rows in total
(\texttt{leaderboard.json.n\_methods}~$=31$), aggregating 257 per-game records
(\texttt{n\_per\_game\_records\_aggregated}~$=257$): 8 methods in Phase~A, 12 in Phase~B, 10
in Phase~C, and the one oracle row. This count is the canonical one used in the abstract,
the figures, and throughout this Supplement.
