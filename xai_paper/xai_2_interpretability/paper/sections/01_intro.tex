Interpretability now matters in practice. Neural networks drive cars, read scans, and
approve loans. Before we trust such a decision, we ask why. Yet on the systems we most
want to explain we cannot check whether the answer is correct. A saliency map for a deep
network can look \emph{convincing} and can change with the action. That does not mean it
delivers \emph{a correct explanation}~\cite{greydanus2018visualizing}. So far, the field
of explainable AI has converged on \emph{faithfulness} as its quality criterion, that is,
whether an explanation names a system's \emph{true} causes. The difficulty is that on a
real neural network there is nothing to measure it against.

Similar shortcomings were also identified in neuroscience. Jonas and Kording showed this
clearly. They applied standard neuroscience methods to a microprocessor in which every
transistor is known~\cite{jonas2017could}. Lesions, tuning curves, and connectomics
produced rich structure, but none of it recovered how the chip computes. Without ground
truth we cannot distinguish a method that finds some structure from one that finds the
\emph{correct} structure. The same blind spot recurs across explainable AI, where
attribution maps are validated against each other or against human intuition rather than
against the system's mechanism~\cite{atrey2020exploratory}. Faithfulness has been used as
a substitute for understanding, but no one could test this substitute.

Maier et al. recently built the missing reference. It is a bit-exact, differentiable port
of the Atari 2600 Video Computer System (VCS) that reproduces a real 1977 machine to the
byte and pixel~\cite{maier2025vcs,xitari}. The VCS is software with a known specification,
so the true cause of any output is computable by exact intervention. This \emph{intervention
oracle} (\Sectionname\ref{sec:oracle}) returns the true causal effect for any candidate
cause of any output, measured rather than estimated. It operates over the platform shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:platform}. On this machine an interpretability claim can be scored right or
wrong.

Interpretability is studied in three communities that rarely cite one another. Yet all
ask the same question: inside a complex system, what caused this output? The neuroscience
tradition records and perturbs a system, through tuning curves, lesions, connectomics, and
directed influence. Jonas and Kording~\cite{jonas2017could} applied these methods to a
microprocessor running classic games. They found that it produces rich, publishable
structure while missing how the chip computes. Their study reported what the analyses found,
but not how much each analysis deviated from the true mechanism. We supply that number
(\Sectionname\ref{sec:results-a}).

The attribution tradition asks, for a single decision, which inputs mattered. Gradient and
saliency maps~\cite{simonyan2014deep,sundararajan2017axiomatic,smilkov2017smoothgrad,springenberg2015striving},
class-activation methods~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam,chattopadhyay2018gradcampp},
occlusion~\cite{zeiler2014visualizing,fong2017interpretable,petsiuk2018rise}, and surrogate
or game-theoretic attributions~\cite{ribeiro2016why,lundberg2017unified} form the toolkit,
applied to Atari agents to visualise what a policy
attends~\cite{greydanus2018visualizing,such2019atarizoo}. Yet a counterfactual analysis of those
same maps found them exploratory, not explanatory~\cite{atrey2020exploratory}: the
highlighted regions need not be the causes. On a learned policy the true cause is unknown,
so this doubt can only be argued. We resolve it on a system where the true cause is
computable (\Sectionname\ref{sec:results-b}).

Mechanistic interpretability, the youngest tradition, aims past attribution at the circuit,
the subgraph that implements a behaviour. Path patching, causal mediation, and scrubbing
intervene on internal
activations~\cite{vig2020investigating,meng2022locating,wang2022interpretability,conmy2023towards};
alignment search and interchange interventions test whether a hypothesised variable is
causally realised~\cite{geiger2021causal,geiger2023finding}; sparse dictionaries and probes
read features off the
state~\cite{cunningham2023sparse,alain2017understanding,hewitt2019designing,belrose2023eliciting,nanda2023attribution}.
On a real artifact these prove the most faithful (\Sectionname\ref{sec:results-c}). Yet the same
oracle shows that a recovered circuit is a correct data-flow graph. It does not give the
function the circuit computes. The sparse-dictionary branch is itself under scrutiny.
SAEBench~\cite{saebench2025} reports that gains on the proxy metrics used to tune sparse
autoencoders do not reliably translate into practical interpretability. We probe the same concern from a different side, asking on a known
system whether a recovered feature is causally used rather than only decodable
(\Sectionname\ref{sec:results-c}).

The agreed criterion across these traditions is faithfulness: claimed causes should be the
true causes. Jacovi and Goldberg~\cite{jacovi2020towards} make it precise, yet its
diagnostics stay indirect when ground truth is absent. Adebayo et
al.~\cite{adebayo2018sanity} sharpened this into the sanity check: a saliency method should
change when weights or labels are randomised, and several popular methods do not. But such
checks are only negative. With no reference map, they cannot certify that a method is
correct. So the field optimises a proxy it cannot directly score. Our oracle supplies that
map and converts faithfulness into a measured quantity.

We study three traditions: neuroscience, attribution, and mechanistic interpretability.
This covers $30$ interpretability methods plus the oracle positive control, across $31$
experiments and $257$ per-game records. We score each method on two axes: faithfulness
against the oracle, and a documented plausibility proxy for how convincing it looks. The
first result is the expected one. Causal and intervention methods are faithful, while
gradient and correlational saliency methods are not. The contrast is sharpest on discrete
sprite-position outputs. There the naive gradient is provably zero, because position is set
by strobe timing rather than by a value it can flow through. In that position regime
activation patching~\cite{vig2020investigating,meng2022locating} reaches faithfulness $1.000$ while vanilla
saliency~\cite{simonyan2014deep} collapses to $0.000$ on all six core games~\cite{faithfuldemoP2}, and
the leaderboard plots all $31$ methods against the same ground truth (Fig.~\ref{fig:faithfulness})~\cite{leaderboardP2}.
The saliency method nonetheless has the higher plausibility
score ($0.9$ versus $0.5$). It looks most convincing exactly where it is least correct.
This is the region Fig.~\ref{fig:faithfulness} marks as conviction without correctness.
This is the result the field would predict, and we treat it as an intermediate step, not
the main finding.

Faithfulness is not what separates good explanations from bad ones. A reader could dismiss
the zero of the gradient methods as a fixable artifact of a discrete index. So we test the
strongest version of the question and close this objection. We turn the differentiable
port's bilinear sampler on. The sampler interpolates the position read and restores a
non-zero gradient through it, and the gradient methods become faithful too. On the games
with a moving tracked sprite their faithfulness rises off the provable zero. Vanilla
saliency, grad$\times$input, smoothgrad, and expected gradients reach $0.791$ on Pong, and
the same family reaches $0.529$ on Q*bert (Fig.~\ref{fig:sampler}). The increase is real but does
not occur on every game. Integrated gradients with a zeros baseline still vanishes on Pong.
A static-sprite game offers no position to restore, and where the tracked cell is not the
active one nothing moves. Yet on every game where the gradient becomes faithful, the method
still recovers no meaning, just as no other method does, faithful or not. The
restored gradient concentrates on the position RAM byte that drives the sprite,
\texttt{ram[54]} on Pong. It identifies the correct cause but supplies no meaning for it.

Securing faithfulness is necessary, but it does not solve the main problem. Faithfulness is
necessary for understanding but far from sufficient. Making it universal does not close the
gap, because the gap is not a faithfulness deficit. With the same oracle we ask not only
\emph{which} methods are faithful but \emph{what} a faithful method delivers. The answer is
always the same kind of object: a true causal-effect map or data-flow graph. It is never
the variable's meaning and never the routine's algorithm. No method on this fully
observable, fully differentiable machine names ``missile,'' ``collision,'' or ``game
restart.'' Every method returns the same wiring fact: change input $X$ and outputs $X$ and
$Y$ move. To make this precise we call an explanation right only if it is \textbf{F}aithful
(its claimed causes are the true ones), \textbf{S}ufficient (it regenerates behavior under
held-out interventions), and \textbf{M}inimal at the algorithmic level
($\mathrm{right} = \mathbf{F}\wedge\mathbf{S}\wedge\mathbf{M}$, \Sectionname\ref{sec:triad}).
The committed data show the gap in both directions. An automatically discovered circuit on
Breakout is graph-correct and minimal yet does not reproduce the behavior it explains
($\mathbf{F}=1.0$, $\mathbf{M}=1.0$, $\mathbf{S}=0.44$ on Breakout). A correct wiring
diagram is not the computed function. A learned feature dictionary on Pong matches a named
game variable perfectly (matched fraction $1.0$) while being causally almost inert
($\mathbf{F}=0.04$ on Pong). Looking like a known variable is not the same as computing it
the way the program does. A concept can be linearly decodable yet provably unused. Being
present in the state does not mean being used. On this machine we can measure the gap
between a faithful attribution and an account of the computation as a number, which we name
the \emph{semantic gap}. It does not shrink when faithfulness is made universal.

The reference for meaning, then, lies outside the mechanism. Every semantic label in the
study entered from outside the system. It was imported from external annotation
sets~\cite{anand2019unsupervised,delfosse2023ocatari} and only \emph{causally verified} on
our substrate, never \emph{discovered} by a method under test. An alignment method that
reports perfect accuracy is therefore right by construction, since it checks a variable we
supplied. Probing shows that information is present, not that it is
used~\cite{hewitt2019designing}, and our oracle is the strictly stronger test. What fixes
the meaning is the system's \emph{behavior}. For software, that behavior is set down in its
documentation and data: the manuals, the specification, and the design. IEEE~610.12 makes
these part of the definition of software itself~\cite{ieee610}. The wiring is in the
mechanism, so any faithful method can recover it. The meaning is not, and no method we
tested supplied it.

The concepts we score against are external, and we are explicit about where they come from.
AtariARI labels object positions, scores, and
lives from commented disassemblies as a probing benchmark~\cite{anand2019unsupervised};
OCAtari adds offset corrections so the coordinates align with the
render~\cite{delfosse2023ocatari}. These labels are imported, not discovered. We verify
them causally by intervention: set the byte, re-render, confirm the object moves to its
predicted place on the bit-exact framebuffer. This step states our honesty contract. Every
concept-level score measures alignment to a supplied label. It does not measure a
meaning the method found on its own. Note that a concept can be linearly decodable yet play
no causal role. Control tasks were built to catch this ``present $\neq$ used''
trap~\cite{hewitt2019designing}. Our intervention test is stronger than probing, and that
separation is one of the three counted in Fig.~\ref{fig:taxonomy}.

Ours is not the first study to score interpretability against a known answer, yet existing
benchmarks build the answer into the system. Yang and Kim's BAM~\cite{yang2019bim} grades
attribution maps against images with known relative feature importance, and
M4~\cite{m4_2023} extends this idea into a faithfulness benchmark spanning metrics,
modalities, and models; in both, the feature ground truth is synthetic, installed by the
data-generation or training procedure. Tracr~\cite{lindner2023tracr} compiles programs into
transformers with circuits known by construction; InterpBench~\cite{gupta2024interpbench}
trains semi-synthetic transformers to a specified circuit. The Mechanistic Interpretability
Benchmark~\cite{mib2025} pushes furthest in our direction, scoring circuit localization and
causal-variable localization against curated answers in neural language models. Yet each of
these answers is engineered for the benchmark, whereas our subject is a real, human-written
artifact whose ground truth is independent of it. The VCS was never designed to be
interpretable. Even so, an exhaustive intervention oracle can recover its full causal
structure, on a bit-exact, differentiable port~\cite{maier2025vcs}, built on the Atari
substrate~\cite{bellemare2013arcade} and the reference emulator~\cite{xitari}. Our novelty is
not the Atari environment, which the Arcade Learning Environment and its
descendants~\cite{bellemare2013arcade,machado2018revisiting} already supply, and which faster
and object-centric variants such as CuLE~\cite{dalton2020cule}, OCAtari and
JAXAtari~\cite{delfosse2023ocatari,delfosse2025jaxatari} extend. The novelty is the
bit-exact, differentiable intervention oracle that turns a known machine into a ground truth
for interpretability. More importantly, the engineered benchmarks validate faithfulness only,
because in them the circuit is defined to be the meaning. On a real artifact the meaning is a
separate object from the causal structure. This lets us separate faithfulness from
understanding. We can show that a method which perfectly recovers the true wiring still has
not recovered the semantics (Fig.~\ref{fig:taxonomy}). We measure that remaining gap, which we
call the semantic gap. Closing it means recovering a program's variables and design from
scratch. We are explicit that this is a different problem, the subject of companion work. To our
knowledge, this is the first cross-tradition audit of these methods on a real human-written
software artifact with an exact intervention oracle; we claim no unqualified ``first ground-truth
calibration,'' since synthetic known-answer benchmarks and Jonas and Kording's microprocessor
study~\cite{jonas2017could} precede us.

Our use of ``interpretable'' is one of several in circulation. We operationalise it as
ground-truth recovery: an explanation is interpretable to the degree that its claimed
structure matches the system's true causal structure, scored by the oracle. Barbiero et
al.~\cite{barbiero2025neural} instead define it as inference equivariance, a design target
for new models, while ours audits a system already known. Marr's levels of
analysis~\cite{marr1982vision} and Lazebnik's ``fix-the-radio''
parable~\cite{lazebnik2002can} support this paper's main point. A faithful
description of a system's wiring is not yet an account of the algorithm it runs. On a
known machine, we measure that gap.

A 1977 chip may seem the wrong place to judge methods built for learned networks. We
read the VCS as a \emph{necessary-condition screen}, not a predictor of success. Here we
have perfect ground truth and no learning obscuring the mechanism. Failure under these
conditions is strong negative evidence for the claim that the same method reliably recovers
causal mechanisms in harder, less observable systems. The VCS still exhibits the properties
that make interpretation hard, from aliased RAM cells to race-the-beam timing.

Because the central claim is sharp, we are deliberate about what we do not claim. We do
\emph{not} claim that any method recovered the program's meaning, algorithm, or design
from scratch, and the plausibility axis is a documented proxy, not a measured human study.
As such, this paper \emph{defines and measures} the semantic gap and locates its reference
in the system's behavior. \emph{Closing} the gap means recovering a system's documentation
and design from the system alone. That is future work, hopefully inspired by this paper.

The study thus offers a reusable ground-truth benchmark for explainable AI, mechanistic
interpretability, AI safety, and software verification, and it reframes the open problem.
The hard part of interpretability is not faithfulness. It is recovering meaning, which
remains unsolved even when faithfulness is achieved. We believe that naming and demonstrating the existence of
this gap is a first step toward closing it.
